Targeting Fungal Hsp90: The Challenge of Selective Chaperone Inhibition

Created on June 08, 2026

2026   ·   drug-discovery   fungal-hsp90   molecular-chaperones   resistance   ·   notebook

Systemic fungal infections represent a silent but growing global health crisis. As pathogens like Candida albicans, Aspergillus fumigatus, and Cryptococcus neoformans evolve resistance to the limited arsenal of available clinical antifungals (azoles, echinocandins, and polyenes), the need for novel therapeutic strategies has become acute.

One of the most promising approaches is targeting the molecular chaperone Heat Shock Protein 90 (Hsp90). Rather than killing the fungus directly, Hsp90 inhibition can compromise the stress response pathways that enable resistance, restoring efficacy to existing drugs.

Hsp90 as a Governor of Resistance

In fungi, Hsp90 acts as a master regulator of cellular circuitry. It stabilizes key client proteins involved in signal transduction, morphogenetic transitions, and stress adaptation. Under normal conditions, Hsp90 keeps these signaling networks in check. Under environmental or pharmacological stress, Hsp90’s chaperone activity enables the fungus to tolerate the stress and survive.

Hsp90 does not create resistance; it chaperones it. By stabilizing mutant or unstable signaling proteins, Hsp90 buffers genetic variation and allows the rapid deployment of stress-tolerant phenotypes.

When Hsp90 is inhibited:

  • The stress response regulator calcineurin and the MAP kinase Mkc1 are degraded.
  • The fungus loses the ability to respond to cell wall or cell membrane damage.
  • Previously resistant strains become hypersensitive to azoles and echinocandins.

The Conservation Dilemma

The therapeutic potential of Hsp90 inhibitors (such as geldanamycin, 17-AAG, and synthetic resorcinol derivatives) has been extensively validated in vitro and in animal models. However, translating these findings to the clinic faces a monumental hurdle: evolutionary conservation.

Hsp90 is a highly conserved protein across all eukaryotes. The N-terminal ATP-binding domain—which is the target of most classical Hsp90 inhibitors—shares roughly 60% to 80% sequence identity between humans and pathogenic fungi.

Human Hsp90:  [ N-Terminal (ATP-binding) ] --- [ Middle Domain ] --- [ C-Terminal ]
Fungal Hsp90: [ N-Terminal (ATP-binding) ] --- [ Middle Domain ] --- [ C-Terminal ]
                   (60-80% Sequence Identity)

Systemic inhibition of human Hsp90 is toxic, leading to severe side effects such as hepatotoxicity, cardiotoxicity, and retinal damage (as observed in oncology trials for Hsp90 inhibitors). For an antifungal therapy to be clinically viable, the inhibitor must show extreme selectivity for the fungal chaperone over its human ortholog.

Strategies for Selective Targeting

To overcome the conservation barrier, computational and structural biology studies are focused on exploiting subtle differences in chaperone structure and dynamics.

1. Exploiting ATP-Binding Pocket Divergence

While the active site residues are almost identical, the overall shape and flexibility of the ATP-binding pocket differ. Structural studies have revealed that the fungal pocket possesses distinct hydrophobic subpockets that can accommodate bulkier or chemically distinct substituents. By designing compounds that physically clash with human Hsp90 residues while fitting snugly into the fungal pocket, selectivity can be achieved.

2. Targeting the Middle and C-Terminal Domains

The middle domain (MD) and C-terminal domain (CTD) of Hsp90 are less conserved than the N-terminal ATP-binding pocket. These domains are responsible for client protein binding, dimerization, and co-chaperone interactions.

  • Disrupting the Hsp90-co-chaperone interface (e.g., between Hsp90 and Aha1 or Cdc37) offers a pathway to block specific stress pathways without shutting down the chaperone’s global housekeeping functions in human cells.

3. Divergent Loop Interactions

Close inspection of the N-terminal domain reveals a few key amino acid differences located on flexible loops near the entrance of the ATP-binding pocket. Designing inhibitors with “extension arms” that interact with these divergent outer residues can provide the thermodynamic drive required for fungal selectivity.

Moving Forward

Targeting fungal Hsp90 represents a paradigm shift from traditional “fungicidal” drugs to “resistance-modifying” agents. By turning Hsp90 off, we strip the pathogen of its evolutionary shield, rendering it vulnerable to the drugs it once ignored.

Success will depend on our ability to navigate the structural nuances of these molecular machines, using high-resolution structural biology and targeted computational screen designs to achieve the selectivity needed to protect human hosts.
